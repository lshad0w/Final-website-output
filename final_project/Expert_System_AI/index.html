Abstract

Republic Act No. 7610, enacted on June 17, 1992, is a landmark Philippine legislation designed to provide stronger deterrence and special protection for children against abuse, exploitation, and discrimination. The Act defines "children" as individuals below 18 years of age or those over 18 who are unable to fully care for themselves due to physical or mental disabilities. It categorizes various forms of child abuse, including physical, psychological, and sexual abuse, as well as neglect, cruelty, and exploitation.

The law mandates the Department of Justice and the Department of Social Welfare and Development, in coordination with other agencies, to develop a comprehensive program aimed at preventing and addressing child abuse, exploitation, and discrimination. This program includes protective services, legal assistance, and rehabilitation for victims.

RA 7610 also outlines penalties for violations and reinforces the state’s authority to intervene when parents, guardians, or custodians fail to protect the child. Ultimately, the Act underscores the state’s unwavering commitment to uphold the rights and welfare of children, with the best interests of the child as the paramount consideration.

Understanding Child Protection

Republic Act No. 7610, or the Special Protection of Children Against Abuse, Exploitation and Discrimination Act, was enacted to provide special protection to children from all forms of abuse, neglect, cruelty, exploitation, and discrimination, as well as other conditions prejudicial to their development. The law mandates state intervention when parents, guardians, or caretakers fail to protect children, and emphasizes that the best interests of the child shall be the paramount consideration in all actions concerning them.

The Act outlines specific forms of child abuse, including physical, sexual, and emotional maltreatment, and defines exploitative situations such as child labor, trafficking, and exposure to obscene materials. It also recognizes children as zones of peace and protects them from threats like armed conflict and hazardous environments. The law establishes comprehensive programs and penalties to prevent abuse and rehabilitate affected children, reinforcing the State’s duty to ensure their survival, development, and dignity.

Source: (The LAWPHiL Project, Republic Act No. 7610)

The Growing Crisis

Alarming Statistics

The Commission on Human Rights (CHR) has raised serious concern over a recent UNICEF report revealing widespread violence against Filipino children. The study found that over 23 million children under 14 faced violent discipline at home, highlighting a troubling culture of violence.

In 2022 alone, nearly 60% of children aged 1 to 14 experienced at least one form of violent discipline. This includes 11.6 million subjected to physical aggression, 9.1 million to physical punishment, and nearly 800,000 who endured severe physical punishment.

Source: (Statement of the Commission on Human Rights on UNICEF’s Report Showing That Over Half of Children in the Country Suffer Violent Discipline – Commission on Human Rights, Philippines, 2024)

Cruel Reality

Child Abuse & Exploitation

Child Trafficking

Child trafficking refers to the act of trading, dealing, buying, or selling children for money, services, or other considerations. It also includes various attempts to commit trafficking, such as facilitating unsanctioned travel abroad for a child without parental or government clearance, recruiting individuals to bear children for sale, simulating a birth record to pass off a child as another's, or seeking children from vulnerable communities with the intent to exploit them.

Child Prostitution and Other Sexual Abuse

This violation involves engaging children in sexual intercourse or lascivious conduct in exchange for money, favors, or due to coercion, often by adults or organized groups. It applies to anyone who promotes or facilitates prostitution, including clients and establishment owners. Attempted child prostitution is also covered, which includes suspicious encounters between a child and an unrelated adult in secluded places or establishments known for such abuse.

Obscene Publications and Indecent Shows

This covers the use of children in obscene performances, whether live or recorded, as well as their inclusion in pornographic publications or media. It also includes the distribution or sale of such materials by minors. Parents or guardians who knowingly allow this kind of exploitation also fall under this violation.

Other Acts of Abuse, Neglect, or Exploitation

This provision covers a broad range of child abuse, cruelty, or exploitation that may not fall under specific offenses like prostitution or trafficking. This includes exposing children to harmful environments, inducing them into illegal acts such as drug trafficking or begging, and allowing them to be in the company of much older individuals in inappropriate places such as motels or bars. It also penalizes those who operate or manage venues where such abuse occurs, as well as those who induce others to commit such acts.

Sanctions Against Establishments

This section applies to any establishment or business that promotes or facilitates child prostitution, trafficking, indecent shows, or similar abuses. It considers the presence of these acts on the premises as evidence of promotion or facilitation, and includes businesses like motels, saunas, travel agencies, and bars.

Illegal Employment of Children

RA 7610 restricts the employment of children below 15 years old, allowing it only under specific exceptions, such as working for their family or participating in media projects with proper government permits. Employers must ensure the child's safety, access to education, and moral development. It also prohibits the use of children in advertisements for alcohol, tobacco, and violence, and mandates non-formal education programs for working children.

Discrimination Against Indigenous Children

This provision prohibits any form of discrimination against children from indigenous cultural communities. It promotes equal treatment and access to services for these children and mandates respect for their customs and traditions.

Children in Situations of Armed Conflict

These provisions protect children affected by armed conflict. The law prohibits their recruitment into armed forces or armed groups and designates them as "zones of peace." It ensures their continued access to education, health, and emergency services, promotes family reunification, and provides special care for children arrested due to their involvement in armed conflict.

Violation of Confidentiality and Media Exposure

This section makes it unlawful to sensationalize cases involving child victims, particularly in media coverage that can cause moral degradation. The law requires that the identity of the child be kept confidential until the court takes jurisdiction over the case.

Remedial Procedures and Legal Process Violations

These provisions ensure the proper handling of child abuse cases. They specify who may file complaints, how protective custody should be administered, and how courts must prioritize these cases. They also outline legal protections for those acting on behalf of the child and detail conditions for confidentiality and court hearings.
